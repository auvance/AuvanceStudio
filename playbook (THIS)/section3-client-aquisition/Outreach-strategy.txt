### What You Need Before Outreach

</aside>

1. **Clean Digital Presence**

Before a brand ever replies to your DM or email, they’re going to **click on your profile**. That’s your first impression - and it needs to be solid.

Minimum requirements:

- **Instagram:**
    - Clear bio (who you help + how)
    - Pinned work, projects, or case studies
    - Branded highlight covers (optional, but clean)
    - Posts that reflect your *style, quality, and niche*
- **Website / Landing Page:**
    - Clear service offer
    - Simple “about” section
    - 1–2 case studies (ideally tailored to the niche you're targeting)
    - Strong contact CTA
- **LinkedIn (optional but powerful):**
    - Headline that reflects what you do
    - Banner with positioning
    - Highlights of work with notable brands or projects

You don’t need a massive archive of work — **2 solid case studies** is enough to start getting traction. If they’re strong, niche-relevant, and clearly demonstrate value, they’ll do 90% of the heavy lifting.

> Think: “This person clearly knows what they’re doing. Their work matches our vibe. They seem legit.”
> 

That’s the bar.

Outreach Strategy




<aside>

8 steps I’ve refined after 6+ years of outreach

</aside>

<aside>

This isn’t a robotic cold email sequence. It’s a lean, human approach that starts with real context and ends with real clients. The structure stays the same across creative fields. If you follow these steps consistently you will start seeing more responses and more Yes´s.

</aside>

<aside>

**Warm Outreach** → Personalized, intentional, and targeted. This is when you’ve done your research, reference something specific about the brand, and tailor your message. It takes more time, but the response rate is way higher - and it’s usually the best way to land premium clients.

**Cold Outreach** → Broad, generic, and volume-driven. This is the “spray and pray” approach where you send the same email to 100 people. While you might get a response here and there, it’s not the best strategy for creatives offering high-ticket services.

I’ll always recommend focusing on **warm outreach**. Fewer emails, but higher quality. 

*10 personalized targeted outreaches done right will beat 100 generic ones every single time.*

Outreach isn’t about convincing - it’s about finding the right fit.
The goal is to start conversations, not force outcomes.

</aside>

### 1. Prospecting: Build Your Warm List

</aside>

Start with 10–20 qualified prospects in your niche. (The more the better).

Focus on clients or brands that:

- Match your style
- Are active online (especially IG/LinkedIn)
- Could benefit from your offer

You’re not looking for everyone, you’re looking for 10 “yes, I can actually help them” brands. These will be your outreach targets.

<aside>

### 2. Two Ways to Start the Outreach (DM or Email First?)

</aside>

### **Option 1: Start with Social (IG or LinkedIn DM)**

**My go-to method.**

You’re more likely to get a reply when they can see your profile, your work, and your vibe. This is why I almost always start with Instagram or LinkedIn, not email.

Use a short, genuine message. Be warm. Show context. Don’t ask for the project right away - give value, ask who handles the marketing or creative. That’s it.

**Here are some messages you can model (video content examples):**

**→ Polished but Friendly**

> Hi [Name/Team], I’ll be in [X Location] this [Month] working with a few boutique stays on high-end video and photo content.
> 
> 
> I came across your property and thought it looked stunning—would love to send more info on something we could put together for you that will bring you [X Result].
> 
> Who would be the best person to speak with?
> 

**→ Direct and Warm**

> Hey [Name/Team], I’m heading to [X Location] this [Month] for a few shoots with boutique hotels and stays.
> 
> 
> Your place caught my eye - would love to connect and see if it makes sense to create something special while I’m there that can bring [X Result].
> 
> Is there someone on your team I could email?
> 

**→ Lead Magnet / Free Resource** 

> Hey [First Name],
> 
> 
> I’ve been following [Brand] for a while - love [insert something specific].
> 
> I noticed [problem area] and think you could improve it by [insert value idea].
> 
> I actually put together a free [cheatsheet / guide / checklist / resource / loom] that could help. 
> 
> What would be the best email to send that to?
> 

**→ Example Work** *(great if you already have case studies or content to share)*

> Hey [First Name],
> 
> 
> Really like what [Brand] is doing - especially [insert something specific].
> 
> I noticed [problem area] and think you could improve it by [insert value idea].
> 
> I’m a [your role] and I help [niche/industry] with this exact thing.
> 
> I just did it for [Case Study Brand]. What’s the best email to send over some examples?
> 

**→ Open Loop** *(sparks curiosity, gets them to reply)*

> Hey [First Name],
> 
> 
> Been following [Brand] - love [insert something specific/relevant].
> 
> Noticed [problem area] and I think there’s room to improve it by [insert value idea].
> 
> What’s your current plan for [related problem]?
> 

Once they reply with the email, move to the next step.

### **Option 2: Start with Email → Follow Up with LinkedIn**

If you don’t get a reply from your first email, or if you prefer to go the more professional route from the start, this is the move.

Here’s how it works:

1. Send a short, value-driven email
2. A few days later, send a DM on LinkedIn to the decision maker
    
    > “Hi [Name], I recently sent over an email about a potential [project/service] for [Brand Name], just wanted to make sure it landed. Would love to connect!”
    > 

Why this works:

- People often ignore emails from unknown addresses
- LinkedIn gives them context: they see your name, your profile, your work
- You’re now showing up in two inboxes, not just one

---

**So Which One Should You Use?**

It comes down to your strengths and your target client:

- **If you have a strong IG/LinkedIn that you can leverage → go social-first**
- **If not yet or you are targeting bigger corporate brands → go email-first, then follow up on LinkedIn**

You can test both approaches and see what works best, but the key is the same: don’t just send one message and hope for the best. Stay human, follow up, and keep momentum.

<aside>

### 3. First Email: Keep It Brief & Value-Driven (2 Min Loom)

</aside>

This email isn’t a pitch. It’s a short, clear intro and a way to open the door to a call. Share what you do, why you reached out, and ask to hop on a quick call to share a few ideas.

You can also send a quick 2 min Loom providing value immediately. Going over something that could use improvement, a bottleneck in their business or current strategy, or a strategy that they are missing out on. This will set you apart and is extremely easy to do. 

**Make sure to also add your relevant links (Website, Portfolio, IG, etc)**

**Note:** There’s no magic formula for the “perfect” outreach email that guarantees a response every time. Even with strong positioning, I receive far more “no’s” than “yes’s”, and that’s completely normal. Outreach is a numbers game and just one piece of the bigger puzzle. By following the steps outlined in this module, you’ll significantly increase your chances of starting conversations and getting the right opportunities through the door.

### 4. After Initial Email: Pre-Call Setup: Warm Them Up (VSL + Case Study)

</aside>

Once they agree to a call, this is where you send your **VSL** (short video explaining what you do) + a case study doc or sample work.

If you didn´t send the quick loom in your initial email, you can also send it now. The more value you give upfront, the better. 

This part makes a big difference.

By the time you’re on a call, they already:

- Know who you are
- Understand your process
- See that you’ve done this before

And that saves you lots of time explaining yourself on the actual call.

**Think about it:** Would you pay someone €5,000 you don’t know or trust?

There is not shortcut to relationship building. These steps are essential. 99% of people don´t do this. Its your unfair advantage and what will set you apart from all the others reaching out daily.

<aside>

### 5. Discovery Call: Listen First

</aside>

This is not a sales call, it’s a context call.

Its where you start building personal trust and the relationship. This is essential. 

Listen to what they have to say. 

You want to understand:

- What the brand is struggling with
- What they’ve tried before
- What would actually help them grow

Your job is to see if you can actually help them.

If yes, let them know you’ll put together a tailored proposal and book a follow-up to go over it together.

**Note:**

If this is a warm lead (they already know your work, came from a referral, or were previously interested) — and you have set packages in place — you can close the deal directly on this first call.

But usually, I prefer to go deeper on this call, then bring a custom offer on the next one.

---

<aside>

### 6. Proposal Call (Not Just a PDF)

</aside>

**NEVER just send a proposal via email.**

You’ll lose control of the conversation and likely get ghosted.

Instead, **book a follow-up call (while on the discovery call)** and walk them through the offer live.

On that second call:

- Go over the goals again
- Present the tailored deliverables / solutions
- Break down pricing and timeline
- Address any questions directly

The point is to keep control, provide clarity, and close live on the call.

---

<aside>

### 7. Follow-Up: Stay Top of Mind

</aside>

Ideally you want to close on the proposal call and send the agreement for signing. 

But in case you cant:

If they haven’t replied within 2–3 days after your proposal call, follow up.

Here’s a light-touch way to do it:

> “Hey [Name], just checking in to see if you had a chance to reflect on our chat. Let me know if any questions came up or if you'd like to go over anything again.”
> 

Send a second (and maybe third) reminder spaced out over the next 5–7 days. After that, move on, keep the energy moving forward.

If its a no, its not meant to be. If the sale feels forced, it’s usually not your ideal client and they will be a pain to work with. 

---

<aside>

### 8. Close Call / Message

</aside>

Once you get confirmation that they’re ready to move forward:

- Set expectations
- Lock in dates
- Send agreement

You can hop on a quick call to do this or just do it over message.

From here, you’re officially in client delivery mode. 

The onboarding process begins and you can schedule the onboarding call.

<aside>

**Purpose of the Pre-Call Sequence**

- To prepare the prospect before the discovery call.
- To position you as an authority and build trust.
- To answer FAQs and objections upfront, so the call focuses on listening to their needs and positioning you as the solution.
- To showcase results and process clarity → making you stand out from “just another freelancer.”
</aside>

---

<aside>

### **What the Pre-Call Sequence Looks Like**

</aside>

When a prospect books a call, they automatically receive 1–2 emails through Calendly (or any scheduling tool). These emails should do two things:

1. **Show how you work** (your process/system) (VSL)
2. **Show results** (case studies / previous work)

That’s it. No fluff, no overwhelming them with 10 emails. Just clean, simple, value-packed information that makes them feel they’re about to speak with the person that can solve their problems.

---

<aside>

### The 2 Core Assets You Need

</aside>

**1. VSL (Video Sales Letter) – 2–4 min**

- Purpose: Position yourself as an authority and give clarity on your process.
- Keep it short, structured, and professional.

**Note:** Your VSL should already live on your **website, socials, or landing page**. The pre-call sequence is just about sending it again to make sure they’ve watched it. 

**(VSL Framework in the next part of the module)**

---

**2. Case Study (Video or PDF)**

- Purpose: Show real-world proof.

Can be:

- A short Loom breakdown of a past project (problem → solution → result)
- A simple PDF with visuals + metrics

### Key Reminders

</aside>

- Don’t overwhelm prospects → keep it to **1–2 pre-call emails max**
- Always try to **go over proposals live on a call** (never just send and hope)
- The pre-call sequence makes sure that when they show up, they already see you as an authority
- Rule of thumb: **The bigger the potential deal, the more pre-call prep you should do**
- Don’t overcomplicate it. For most situations, the VSL + Case Study is already enough to build trust and get prospects primed before the call.

By the time someone gets on a call with you, they should already be halfway sold. The VSL does the heavy lifting - so the call becomes a confirmation, not a pitch.

### **VSL Framework: The 10-Step System**

</aside>

### **1. Open With The Pain / Problem**

Start by addressing the exact frustration your ideal client is dealing with. Be specific—generic pain points don't convert.

**Pro Tips:**

- Use language your clients actually use (pull from discovery calls)
- Address the emotional impact, not just the surface problem
- Make it feel like you're reading their mind

**Example:** "You’re putting out content consistently, but nothing is converting. Meanwhile, your competitors with worse properties are booked solid."

---

### **2. Transformation / Claim**

Make a bold, outcome-driven claim that shows what's possible. This should feel almost too good to be true—but believable.

**Example:** "We've built a system that lets us create a full month of content in just 2 hours of your time."

**Framework:** "We help [ideal client] achieve [specific outcome] in [timeframe] without [common struggle]."

**What Makes This Work:**

- Specific timeframe creates urgency
- Addresses their biggest constraint (usually time or expertise)
- Positions you as the solution, not just another service provider

---

### **3. Who Is This For?**

Clarify exactly who your system is designed to help. This makes the right people feel special.

**Example:** "If you're a boutique hotel or villa that wants to attract high-end travelers and keep bookings consistent every single month…"

**Advanced Approach:**

- Include what they value (quality over quantity)
- Mention their current situation (already successful but want growth)
- Add a qualifier that shows you work with serious clients

**Better Example:** "This is for boutique properties that already deliver exceptional experiences but struggle to communicate that online—and are ready to invest in getting it right."

---

### **4. Social Proof / Credibility (Round 1)**

Build immediate trust by mentioning past clients or relevant experience. Don't just name-drop—show relevance.

**Example:** "We've worked with brands like X, X, and a variety of boutique hotels across Europe."

**What to Include:**

- Recognizable names in your niche (if you have them)
- Geographic relevance if location matters
- Scale indicators (number of projects, years of experience)

**Better Example:** "Over the past 3 years, we've created content for 50+ luxury properties across Europe—from Michelin-starred restaurants in Lisbon to private villas in Bali."

---

### **5. Unique Mechanism**

Introduce the "how" behind your results. This is your secret sauce—keep it simple but intriguing.

**Example:** "We do this by leveraging 4 key pillars: cinematic content, ad-ready assets, simple systems, and a seamless client workflow."

**Framework Elements:**

- Give it a name (makes it memorable and proprietary)
- Use 3-4 pillars (easy to remember)
- Each pillar should solve a specific problem

**Example:** "It's called the 'Content Sprint Method'—we compress months of content creation into intensive shoot days, then multiply that content across every platform using our 4-pillar system."

---

### **6. Social Proof / Credibility (Round 2)**

Back up your mechanism with results and client wins. Use specific numbers and outcomes.

**Example:** "For example, we helped a boutique stay in Portugal increase bookings by 3x in just 3 months by simplifying their content strategy."

**What Makes Proof Powerful:**

- Specific metrics (3x, 40% increase, etc.)
- Timeframes (3 months, 6 weeks)
- Similar client to your prospect
- The "how" behind the result

**Better Example:** "Last month, a 12-room boutique hotel in Ericeira went from 60% to 95% occupancy in 8 weeks using content we created in just 2 shoot days—because we focused on showcasing experiences, not just rooms."

---

### **7. Options (Throwing Rocks)**

Show them the alternatives and why they don't work as well. This positions your offer as the obvious choice.

**Example:** "You could hire a big agency for 15k a month… or spend the next year trying to figure out ads and content yourself, while competitors get ahead."

**The Three Alternatives Framework:**

- **Do Nothing:** Stay stuck where you are
- **DIY Route:** Time-consuming and often ineffective
- **Traditional Solution:** Expensive, slow, or impersonal

**Better Example:** "You have three options: Keep posting random content and hoping something works. Hire a big agency that treats you like account #47. Or work with specialists who understand luxury hospitality and can deliver results in weeks, not months."

---

### **8. Value Stack (Benefits)**

List out exactly what they get when they work with you. Focus on outcomes, not just deliverables.

**Example:** "We create done-for-you content packages — from execution to delivery, strategy, and support. You just show up, and we take care of the rest."

**Structure:**

- Lead with the main outcome
- List specific deliverables
- Emphasize the experience (how easy it is for them)

**Better Example:** "You get a complete content system: 2 hero videos that showcase your property's unique story, 40+ photos for every marketing need, 8 social media ads ready to run, plus a content strategy guide so you know exactly how to use everything. All delivered within 7 days of our shoot."

---

### **9. Risk Reversal**

Remove objections by flipping the risk. Make it safer to say yes than to say no.

**Types of Guarantees:**

- **Satisfaction:** Unlimited revisions until happy
- **Performance:** Results-based guarantee (use carefully)
- **Process:** Guarantee the experience will be smooth

---

### **10. Price / CTA**

Close with a clear next step. Make it about them, not you.

**Example:** "If this sounds like something worth exploring, click the link below to book a quick 15-minute call. Worst case, you'll walk away with a free roadmap. Best case, we scale together."

**CTA Framework:**

- Acknowledge their decision-making process
- Offer value even if they don't buy
- Make the next step feel small and non-committal
- Create urgency without being pushy

**Better Example:** "If you're ready to stop guessing what content works and start seeing real bookings from your marketing, let's talk. Book a 15-minute strategy call below—I'll audit your current content and show you exactly what's missing, whether we work together or not."

---

### **Implementation Tips:**

**Where to Use Your VSL:**

- Website homepage or dedicated landing page
- LinkedIn About section (condensed version)
- Instagram highlights (broken into story segments)
- Email sequences for cold outreach
- Pre-call sequence to warm up prospects

**Testing Your VSL:**

- Record yourself delivering it conversationally
- Test different pain points with different client segments
- Track which version gets more call bookings
- Update social proof regularly with new wins

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**

- Making it about you instead of them
- Using industry jargon they don't understand
- Weak or generic social proof
- Unclear or weak call-to-action
- Too long (aim for 3-5 minutes when spoken)

Your VSL should feel like a conversation with your ideal client where you demonstrate you understand their world - and have a clear system to solve their problems.

### **Make It Your Own**

</aside>

This 10-step framework will give you a solid foundation that converts—but remember, it's a starting point, not a rigid formula.

Your niche, personal style, and target clients will influence what works best. A creative business targeting luxury hospitality will use different pain points and social proof than someone focusing on fitness brands or real estate agents.

**Test and adapt:**

- Try different pain points that resonate with your specific niche
- Experiment with the order of elements
- Test shorter vs. longer versions
- See if your prospects respond better to data-driven proof or emotional stories
- Adjust your language to match how your ideal clients actually speak

The framework gives you the structure—your niche expertise, client insights, and testing will make it powerful.

Start here, then make it yours.
